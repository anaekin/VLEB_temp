MIMIC: Multimodal Inversion for Model
Interpretation and Conceptualization

Animesh Jain, Alexandros Stergiou

University of Twente, NL

Paper ArXiv GitHub


Abstract


Vision Language Models (VLMs) encode multimodal inputs over large, complex, and difficult-to-interpret architectures, which limit transparency and trust. We propose a Multimodal Inversion for Model Interpretation and Conceptualization (MIMIC) framework to visualize the internal representations of VLMs by synthesizing visual concepts corresponding to internal encodings. MIMIC uses a joint VLM-based inversion and a feature alignment objective to account for VLM's autoregressive processing. It additionally includes a triplet of regularizers for spatial alignment, natural image smoothness, and semantic realism. We quantitatively and qualitatively evaluate MIMIC by inverting visual concepts over a range of varying-length free-form VLM output texts. Reported results include both standard visual quality metrics as well as semantic text-based metrics. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first model inversion approach addressing visual interpretations of VLM concepts.



Overview




MIMIC Framework


Method. In this section, we provide a detailed explanation of our proposed method. An overview of MIMIC is shown in Fig. 1. We initialize an updatable input \( \widehat{\mathbf{v}} \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times H \times W} \) with \(C\) channels, \(H\) height, and \(W\) width. As VLMs can respond to queries given a multimodal context window, we create a text prompt template \( \mathbf{t} \) for a concept we want to visualize. For example: What is shown in the picture: a. [target] concept, or b. [negative] concept — which can be, e.g., a. tiger and b. dog.

In transformer-based VLMs, text is tokenized by \( \mathcal{G}(\mathbf{t}) \) into a sequence of embeddings. Similarly, vision input \( \widehat{\mathbf{v}} \) is encoded by \( \mathcal{E}_{\theta_e} \) to embeddings \( \mathcal{E}_{\theta_e}(\widehat{\mathbf{v}}) \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times \Omega} \), with \( D \) image tokens and \(\Omega\) channels. Both are combined into a concatenated input: $$ \widehat{\mathbf{x}} = [ \mathcal{G}(\mathbf{t}), \mathcal{E}_{\theta_e}(\widehat{\mathbf{v}}) ] $$

VLM Inversion. The backbone LLM model \( \Phi_{\theta_\phi} \) attends to the multimodal prompt \( \widehat{\mathbf{x}} \). The final layer projects the embeddings to a probabilistic distribution: $$ \widehat{\mathbf{y}} = s(\Phi_{\theta_\phi}(\widehat{\mathbf{x}})) $$ where \( s \) is a softmax function over a fixed-length dictionary.

Due to the LLM’s autoregressive nature, generated logits/text often vary in length \( |\widehat{\mathbf{y}}| \), e.g., The image depicts a tiger lying down. We define an adapted cross-entropy loss \( \mathcal{L}_{SCE} \) given the token index with the highest logit for [target] as: $$ \mathcal{L}_{SCE}(\widehat{\mathbf{y}}) = - \sum \mathbf{1}_{[\text{sg}(\widehat{\mathbf{y}}) = \texttt{[target]}]} \log(\widehat{\mathbf{y}}) $$ where \( \mathbf{1}_{[\cdot]} \) is the indicator function, \( \text{sg}(\cdot) \) denotes stop-gradient, and \( |\widehat{\mathbf{y}}| \) is the sequence length.

Base Feature Loss. To align synthesized images with the internal representations of the vision encoder, we extract per-layer features: $$ \widehat{\mathbf{z}}_l = \mathcal{E}_{\theta_e}(\widehat{\mathbf{v}}, l) $$ and compute their mean and variance across layers \( l \in \Lambda = \{1,\dots,L\} \) compared to reference target statistics \( \bar{\mathbf{Z}}_l \). The base feature loss is given by: $$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{base}} = \sum_{l \in \Lambda} \left( \left\| \mu(\widehat{\mathbf{z}}_l) - \mu(\bar{\mathbf{Z}}_l) \right\|_2^2 + \left\| \sigma(\widehat{\mathbf{z}}_l) - \sigma(\bar{\mathbf{Z}}_l) \right\|_2^2 \right) $$

Triplet Feature Regularizer. As VLMs encode multimodal inputs in complex feature spaces, we enhance optimization with three regularizers inspired by vision-only inversion methods. First, \( \mathcal{R}_{\text{patch}} \) smooths transitions across ViT patch boundaries to improve spatial coherence. Second, we define a composite image prior: $$ \mathcal{R}_{\text{prior}}(\widehat{\mathbf{v}}) = \alpha_1 \mathcal{R}_{TV1}(\widehat{\mathbf{v}}) + \alpha_2 \mathcal{R}_{TV2}(\widehat{\mathbf{v}}) + \alpha_3 \mathcal{R}_{\ell_2}(\widehat{\mathbf{v}}) $$ which includes total variation (TV) and \( \ell_2 \)-norm penalties for smoothness and range control.

Third, the feature distribution regularizer \( \mathcal{R}_V \) encourages alignment with BN feature statistics from a verifier network \( \mathcal{F} \) trained on a dataset \( \mathcal{V} \). We extract mean and variance across BN layers \( k \in N \), and compute: $$ \mathcal{R}_V(\widehat{\mathbf{v}}) = \sum_{k \in N} \left\| \mu_k(\mathcal{F}(\widehat{\mathbf{v}})) - \mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{V}}[\mu_k] \right\|_2^2 + \left\| \sigma_k(\mathcal{F}(\widehat{\mathbf{v}})) - \mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{V}}[\sigma_k] \right\|_2^2 $$

The aggregated regularization objective becomes: $$ \mathcal{R}(\widehat{\mathbf{v}}) = \beta_1 \mathcal{R}_V(\widehat{\mathbf{v}}) + \beta_2 \mathcal{R}_{\text{patch}}(\widehat{\mathbf{v}}) + \mathcal{R}_{\text{prior}}(\widehat{\mathbf{v}}), $$ where \( \beta_1, \beta_2, \alpha_1, \alpha_2, \alpha_3 \) are scaling hyperparameters.

Aggregated Optimization Objective. We iteratively update \( \widehat{\mathbf{v}} \) over steps \( i \rightarrow i+1 \) by minimizing the total objective: $$ \widehat{\mathbf{v}}_{i+1} = \min_{\widehat{\mathbf{v}}_i} \gamma_1 \mathcal{L}_{SCE} \left(s(\Phi_{\theta_\phi}([\mathcal{G}(\mathbf{t}), \mathcal{E}_{\theta_e}(\widehat{\mathbf{v}})]))\right) + \gamma_2 \mathcal{L}_{\text{base}} + \mathcal{R}(\widehat{\mathbf{v}}), $$ where \( \gamma_1, \gamma_2 \) are loss scaling factors. This objective guides reconstructions that reveal internal VLM encodings, making their learned concepts visually interpretable.



Results




We invert LLaVA-1.5 that consists of a CLIP ViT-L/14 vision encoder and a LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct language model. Model parameters remain frozen and the updatable input \( \widehat{\mathbf{v}} \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 336 \times 336} \), initialized from a Gaussian distribution \( \widehat{\mathbf{v}} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1) \), includes the only updatable parameters. We compute \( \mathcal{L}_{\text{base}} \) from ImageNet. Optimization is run for 5000 iterations per concept using Adam, with a grid search over \( \{a_1, a_2, a_3, \beta_1, \beta_2, \gamma_1, \gamma_2\} \) and fixed random seed \( \in \{0, 1, 2\} \) for reproducibility.

Baseline results of our method's performance across configurations are shown in Table 1. The aggregated objective achieves a balance across metrics, with 41.25% Top-1 accuracy, and CLIPScore of 27.42 Structure-based regularizers \( \mathcal{R}_{\text{patch}} \) and \( \mathcal{R}_{\text{prior}} \) show the largest improvements across image quality metrics (IS\(_\infty\), FID\(_\infty\)). Similarly, \( \mathcal{L}_{\text{base}} \) and \( \mathcal{R}_{V} \) primarily improve semantic metrics such as top-1/5 accuracy and CLIPScore.

Qualitative results show improvements across semantic, perceptual, and distributional metrics with each objective component. Qualitatively, visualizations show that \( \mathcal{R}_{\text{patch}} \) and \( \mathcal{R}_{\text{prior}} \) enhance smoothness and alignment, while \( \mathcal{L}_{\text{base}} \) and \( \mathcal{R}_V \) guide feature-level fidelity. Additional experiments on varying output lengths \( |\widehat{\mathbf{y}}| \) demonstrate that image quality remains visually consistent, confirming the robustness of our approach.
